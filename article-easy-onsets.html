Easy Onsets: How to Start Words Without Blocking

Fluent Minds · January 12, 2025 · 7 min read

Easy onsets — also called gentle voice onsets — are one of the most effective fluency techniques for people who stutter on vowel sounds at the start of words. Instead of trying to "push through" a block, you learn to initiate your voice gradually, which prevents the tension that causes the stutter in the first place.

📖 What the research says

Easy onset speech refers to the slow initiation of vocal fold vibration (Max & Caruso, 1997). Sudden or forceful vocal fold movement is a key contributor to stuttering on words that begin with vowels — easy onsets address this directly at the source.

Why Do We Block on Vowels?

When you speak, your vocal folds (the two small muscle folds in your larynx) vibrate together to produce voiced sounds. Most consonants give the vocal folds a gentle "run-up" — for example, the sound /s/ in "sorry" is voiceless, so by the time the vocal folds need to vibrate for the vowel that follows, they're already in motion.

Vowel sounds at the start of words don't have that advantage. The vocal folds go from a fully closed, resting position to suddenly needing to vibrate — all at once. For people who stutter, this abrupt demand creates excess tension and can trigger a block or repetition.

Easy onsets solve this by teaching you to start with a tiny breath of air (a quiet "h" sound) before turning on your voice. This gives the vocal folds a gentle, gradual start instead of a sudden jolt.

💡 Quick check

Place your fingers lightly on your voice box (Adam's apple area) and say a long "ahhhh." You'll feel the vibration. Now say "after" — notice how your voice folds must "switch on" immediately. That abrupt switch is what easy onsets help you manage.

Step-by-Step Exercises

Work through these three stages in order. Don't rush — spend at least a few days on each stage before moving to the next.

1
Start with Vowel Sounds Alone
  • 1
    Breathe out with a quiet, relaxed sigh. You should hear a soft "hhhhh" sound — not harsh, just a gentle breath. Your voice is not yet "on."
  • 2
    While still exhaling, gradually turn on your voice to produce a vowel sound. It should sound like "hhhhheee" — the voice fades in slowly.
  • 3
    The key is that the "h" before the vowel is stretched and audible. This is exaggerated on purpose — you'll refine it later.
  • 4
    Practice with each of these vowel sounds: ay, ah, ee, oh, oo, ow
2
Apply to Single Words

Now apply the same technique to individual words starting with vowels. The word begins with a quiet breath-h, then the voice turns on gradually.

Anna→ "hhhh-anna"
After→ "hhhh-after"
Always→ "hhhh-always"
Enough→ "hhhh-enough"
Email→ "hhhh-email"
Either→ "hhhh-either"
Inside→ "hhhh-inside"
Open→ "hhhh-open"
Uncle→ "hhhh-uncle"
Update→ "hhhh-update"
Offer→ "hhhh-offer"
Expert→ "hhhh-expert"
💡 Tip

Record yourself on your phone and listen back. Check that you can clearly hear the gentle "h" before the vowel. If the onset sounds hard or abrupt, slow it down more.

3
Practice Full Phrases

Now move to phrases. Apply the easy onset to the first word of each phrase. The rest of the sentence can be spoken normally.

  • "hhhhhExcuse me…" — then continue naturally
  • "hhhhhOf course."
  • "hhhhhAfter you."
  • "hhhhhIt's a good day."
  • "hhhhhAre you busy?"
  • "hhhhhI'll see you later."
  • "hhhhhOpen the door, please."
  • "hhhhhIs this okay?"

Also write your own! Think of phrases you say every day that start with vowels — especially ones where you know you sometimes block.

Making It Less Noticeable

At first, your easy onset will be very exaggerated — a long, obvious "hhhh" before every vowel. That's intentional. Once you're comfortable with the technique, you can start reducing the length and volume of the "h" sound.

The goal is to keep just enough of the breath-onset to prevent the hard start, while making it quiet enough that most listeners won't notice. Think of it as a very brief exhale — almost like a tiny, silent sigh — that you slip in before the vowel.

✅ Practice approach

Record yourself each practice session. Compare your early recordings (exaggerated onset) to your recent ones (refined onset). Hearing your own progress is one of the most motivating tools available.

How This Relates to Other Techniques

Easy onsets are often taught alongside soft contacts — a similar technique for consonant sounds. Where easy onsets handle vowel-initial words, soft contacts handle words starting with consonants like "p," "b," or "t" — sounds that require the lips or tongue to make firm contact and then release.

Together, these two techniques cover almost every word in the language. Many speech therapists teach them as a pair for this reason.

The goal is not to eliminate your stutter — it's to remove the fear and tension that make it worse. Easy onsets give your voice a safe way to begin.

Summary

Easy onsets work by replacing a sudden, tense vocal onset with a gradual, breath-initiated one. They're most useful for words and phrases that begin with vowel sounds. With regular practice — even just 10 minutes a day — most people notice a real reduction in blocks on vowel-initial words within a few weeks.

The three stages are: (1) practice with isolated vowel sounds, (2) apply to single words, (3) extend to full phrases. Once you're confident, gradually reduce the exaggeration until the technique becomes natural and nearly invisible.

References

Max, L. & Caruso, A. J. (1997). Contemporary techniques for establishing fluency in the treatment of adults who stutter. Contemporary Issues in Communication Science and Disorders, 24, 45–52.
